<s> You need a dial-up modem for dial up, analog service. </s>
<s> You need a NIC to use digital service. </s>
<s> The NIC is not a modem but an interface between the cable or DSL modem and the PC. </s>
<s> The ISP signal is delivered to the modem (in reality an analog signal with cable and DSL) and this signal is converted to a fully digital signal with separate transmit and receive paths in the Ethernet cord (8 pin). </s>
<s> The NIC receives this signal from the modem and delivers it to the PC, and from the PC back to the modem. </s>
<s> The NIC can stand alone connected to the cable or DSL modem. </s>
<s> Or the modem can be connected to a router and thus connect several PCs with NICs and all share the connection to the internet. </s>
<s> And each PC can talk to the other PCs on your ''Home Network''. </s>

<s> Here's some &quot;stuff&quot;: An identifier for a computer or device on a TCP/IP network. </s>
<s> Networks using the TCP/IP protocol route messages based on the IP address of the destination. </s>
<s> The format of an IP address is a 32-bit numeric address written as four numbers separated by periods. </s>
<s> Each number can be zero to 255. </s>
<s> For example, 1.160.10.240 could be an IP address. </s>
<s> Within an isolated network, you can assign IP addresses at random as long as each one is unique. </s>
<s> However, connecting a private network to the Internet requires using registered IP addresses (called Internet addresses) to avoid duplicates. </s>
<s> The four numbers in an IP address are used in different ways to identify a particular network and a host on that network. </s>
<s> Four regional Internet registries -- ARIN, RIPE NCC, LACNIC and APNIC -- assign Internet addresses from the following three classes. </s>
<s> Class A - supports 16 million hosts on each of 126 networks Class B - supports 65,000 hosts on each of 16,000 networks Class C - supports 254 hosts on each of 2 million networks The number of unassigned Internet addresses is running out, so a new classless scheme called CIDR is gradually replacing the system based on classes A, B, and C and is tied to adoption of IPv6. </s>
<s> ---------------------- Every computer that communicates over the Internet is assigned an IP address that uniquely identifies the device and distinguishes it from other computers on the Internet. </s>
<s> An IP address consists of 32 bits, often shown as 4 octets of numbers from 0-255 represented in decimal form instead of binary form. </s>
<s> For example, the IP address 168.212.226.204 in binary form is 10101000.11010100.11100010.11001100. </s>
<s> But it is easier for us to remember decimals than it is to remember binary numbers, so we use decimals to represent the IP addresses when describing them. </s>
<s> However, the binary number is important because that will determine which class of network the IP address belongs to. </s>
<s> An IP address consists of two parts, one identifying the network and one identifying the node, or host. </s>
<s> The Class of the address determines which part belongs to the network address and which part belongs to the node address. </s>
<s> All nodes on a given network share the same network prefix but must have a unique host number. </s>
<s> Class A Network -- binary address start with 0, therefore the decimal number can be anywhere from 1 to 126. </s>
<s> The first 8 bits (the first octet) identify the network and the remaining 24 bits indicate the host within the network. </s>
<s> An example of a Class A IP address is 102.168.212.226, where &quot;102&quot; identifies the network and &quot;168.212.226&quot; identifies the host on that network. </s>
<s> Class B Network -- binary addresses start with 10, therefore the decimal number can be anywhere from 128 to 191. </s>
<s> (The number 127 is reserved for loopback and is used for internal testing on the local machine.) </s>
<s> The first 16 bits (the first two octets) identify the network and the remaining 16 bits indicate the host within the network. </s>
<s> An example of a Class B IP address is 168.212.226.204 where &quot;168.212&quot; identifies the network and &quot;226.204&quot; identifies the host on that network. </s>
<s> Class C Network -- binary addresses start with 110, therefore the decimal number can be anywhere from 192 to 223. </s>
<s> The first 24 bits (the first three octets) identify the network and the remaining 8 bits indicate the host within the network. </s>
<s> An example of a Class C IP address is 200.168.212.226 where &quot;200.168.212&quot; identifies the network and &quot;226&quot; identifies the host on that network. </s>
<s> Class D Network -- binary addresses start with 1110, therefore the decimal number can be anywhere from 224 to 239. </s>
<s> Class D networks are used to support multicasting. </s>
<s> Class E Network -- binary addresses start with 1111, therefore the decimal number can be anywhere from 240 to 255. </s>
<s> Class E networks are used for experimentation. </s>
<s> They have never been documented or utilized in a standard way. </s>
